# Human texture — the seasoning layer

Anti-slop is the foundation. This is the seasoning. Read this when you want copy to read like a
specific person wrote it, not like a competent machine — social posts, newsletters, essays, even a
warm email.

---

## Foundation vs seasoning (read this first)

Two layers, applied in order. They don't contradict — they stack.

- **Anti-slop = the foundation (the floor).** It strips the patterns a model emits on autopilot:
  negative parallelism, "let that sink in," *delve / leverage / unlock*, the forced rule of three,
  reflexive analogies, metronome rhythm. This is always on. It is not negotiable. See
  [anti-slop.md](anti-slop.md).
- **Humanizing = the seasoning (the layer on top).** Once the slop is gone, you add deliberate texture
  so the prose has a pulse: a tangent, a fragment, an undercut, the real unflattering thought. This is
  a conscious craft choice, applied with judgment and taste.

**Why they're not in conflict:** slop is what comes out when the model *stops thinking* and starts
spewing tokens. Seasoning is what a writer *adds on purpose*. The banned patterns are banned because
they're reflexive filler that carries no content — not because "texture" is bad. Texture is the goal.

**The test that separates them** — ask of any flourish:

> Did I *choose* this, and does it carry something only I would say? Or is it a reflexive pattern I'd
> emit without thinking?

Intentional + content-bearing → season it in. Reflexive + empty → it's slop, cut it.

**The sequence:**
1. Write the thing plainly (say something true and specific).
2. Strip the slop (run [anti-slop.md](anti-slop.md)).
3. Season for humanity (this file).

Never season to *cover up* slop. Foundation first. A vivid aside on top of an empty sentence is still
an empty sentence.

## The moves (worked examples — study Shaan Puri)

Shaan Puri's "One Minute Blog" essays are a master class in human-feeling short prose. Study the
**moves**; set them to Sid's register (Shaan's persona — slang, profanity, NBA/celebrity palette — is
his, not ours; the *techniques* transfer cleanly). Full teardown + index: `positioning/swipe/posts/shaan-puri/`.

Several of these extend principles already in [voice-principles.md](voice-principles.md) (parenthetical
personality, sentence rhythm, acknowledge the reader, say it out loud). This file is the deep, worked-
example version.

### 1. Talk to the reader — and answer the objection they're already forming
Write at the reader, then voice the pushback in their head and take it on.

> "Let's see if this describes you:" — *someday* (opening line)

> "But I know what you're thinking. What game should I even play? How do I find a game I love?" — *the 3-step plan*

> "You might think that's foolish. Why agree to things so quickly? Shouldn't you think things through?" — *down*

**Why it works:** it turns a monologue into a conversation. The reader feels met, not lectured.
**For Sid:** keep it; this is already in voice-principles ("acknowledge the reader's position"). One beat, then move.

### 2. Undercut your own authority
Right when you could sound like a guru, puncture it.

> "Remember, I'm just a jackass with wifi." — *the 3-step plan*

> "I'll give you a car analogy (which is dangerous, because I'm the 1 man on Earth who knows absolutely nothing about cars… but I'll give it a try)" — *i got internet famous…*

**Why it works:** self-deprecation buys trust. He's beside you, not above you.
**For Sid:** keep the move, lose the slang. "I might be wrong here" or "I've only run this twice" does the same job in a clean register. (Don't tip into performative humility — voice-principles bans "I'm just a simple founder.")

### 3. Parenthetical asides (the side-channel thought)
The aside *is* the personality — the thing the writer couldn't help but mutter.

> "(sidebar: I wonder if Idaho whispers, 'your potato's suck')" — *proximity is power*

> "(trust me, you don't want your doctor 'getting creative' on you…)" — *the 3-step plan*

> "(Even Nick Cannon paying child support for 11 kids got a better deal than that)." — *elon musk*

**Why it works:** a perfectly linear argument reads like a robot. The detour signals a mind at work.
**For Sid:** 1–2 per social post, 2–4 per essay (voice-principles limit). The aside should carry a real thought or a dry joke, not just chatter.

### 4. Break rhythm — hard
Follow a long sentence with a two-word fragment. Let a single line stand alone.

> "He just wants to build things. Cars. Rockets. Tunnels." — *elon musk*

> "Just ignored. Nothing, nada, zilch." — *power brokers*

> "Challenge accepted." — *creativity <3's constraints* (one line, by itself)

**Why it works:** rhythm is what the ear hears. Uniform sentence length is the #1 tell of machine prose.
**For Sid:** the workhorse is still the medium sentence (~55%); fragments *punctuate*, they don't dominate (voice-principles). If every line is a fragment, you've made a new kind of slop.

### 5. Concrete sensory detail (specificity as texture, not just proof)
Specifics don't only prove a claim — they make the reader *see* it.

> "I step to the free throw line.. **dribble..dribble.. exhale…CLANK!**" — *mispriced assets*

> "Costco frozen grilled chicken bag ($25 for 10 pounds of chicken), Eggs, Avocados (our luxury item), Rice, Ranch style beans, Tons (and I mean, tons) of frank's red hot sauce" — *strategically broke*

> "He thought a bag of Totino's pizza rolls was $22." — *strategically broke*

**Why it works:** the exact brand, number, and sound are proof you were actually there. Vague = invented.
**For Sid:** this is squarely on-brand (voice-principles "specificity as proof"). Pull the detail from the real work — the token cost, the tool name, the exact step.

### 6. Render the unflattering inner monologue
Quote the embarrassing thought, not the polished takeaway.

> "So I clicked 'play' and listened to the first minute myself. 'Ew. Is that what I sound like? Why am I saying "like" so many times? This sucks. I hope nobody hears this.'" — *power brokers*

> "At the time, I felt like I was striking out. Over and over again. But I was having fun, so I kept going." — *strategically broke*

**Why it works:** nobody's inner voice is a LinkedIn caption. The real thought is the fastest proof a person is behind the words.
**For Sid:** keep it honest and specific to the moment. One real thought beats a paragraph of reflection.

### 7. Break the fourth wall — admit the bit
Call out your own move while doing it.

> "Marcus Aurelius once said… Seneca believed… **Actually, they didn't.** But I told ChatGPT to 'make it sound like it came from 10 wise philosophers'…" — *the 3-step plan*

> "And look, I get it. I'm 1,100 words in, and all I've said is 30 different flavors of 'omg, i love Elon'." — *elon musk*

> "OK this is getting long. But you're still here. Weird, right? That's because there's no such thing as too long, only 'too boring'." — *i got internet famous…*

**Why it works:** showing the seams reads as honesty. You trust the reader enough to admit the trick.
**For Sid:** use sparingly. One self-aware line per piece, max. It's seasoning, not the meal.

### 8. Quote real dialogue
Let people talk. A line of real speech beats a summary of it.

> The coach: "Oh man, you got me. That was good. That was so good. That was my favorite moment at work this whole year." — *mispriced assets*

> Ben, after agreeing on the spot: "huh? sorry I wasn't listening, I just went to GoDaddy buying the domain so I can setup the site tonight" — *down*

**Why it works:** speech has texture that paraphrase sands off. It also moves the story without narration.
**For Sid:** quote the client, the prospect, the engineer — the actual words from a real call or thread. (Honor the evidence rule: only quote what was really said.)

### 9. Earned comparison (the intentional kind)
Shaan reaches for a vivid image constantly. Most are *persona* (and most reflexive analogies are slop —
that's why anti-slop gates them). But the deliberate, clarifying comparison is seasoning:

> "Safety is a serial killer. But it operates in a slow, boring fashion. Like a pillow suffocating an old-man on a hospital bed." — *someday*

**Why it works (when it works):** a surprising, exact image makes an abstract idea physical and sticky.
**For Sid:** this is the one place to stay disciplined. Run the **permission test** (anti-slop): is the
subject abstract, does the image clarify, is it shorter than the literal version, exact, and normal read
aloud? A deliberate image that passes = seasoning. A reflexive "it's like a journey/engine/bridge" =
slop. Frequency limits still apply (0 under 800 words).

### 10. Spoken diction
He writes the way he'd say it — contractions, interjections, the occasional sound effect.

> "Back in the 1950s — Dr. Seuss was on a heater." — *creativity <3's constraints*
> "Wow." / "What?" — *strategically broke* / *stop working so hard* (standalone reactions)

**Why it works:** it passes the "say it out loud" test by construction (voice-principles).
**For Sid:** keep the contractions and the read-aloud cadence; drop the slang and profanity. Clean and
spoken, not casual-bro. The target is "smart friend explaining it at a bar," minus the bar.

### 11. The signature sign-off + turn it on the reader
End by handing the idea to the reader, then sign with a consistent mark.

> "So, who's your Elon?" — *who's your Elon?* · "So go call your mom today!" — *don't be an asshole…*
> Signature: "-Uncle Shaan" / "Stay thirsty, Uncle Shaan."

**Why it works:** the close makes the reader *do* something with the idea; the signature builds a ritual.
**For Sid:** end on the most specific or open note (voice-principles "endings that open"). A consistent
sign-off can become a brand asset — pick one that fits Sid's voice, not a borrowed catchphrase.

## Shaan's full writing system (the teardown)

The moves above sit inside a repeatable system. (Condensed from `positioning/swipe/posts/shaan-puri/ANALYSIS.md`.)

### The named, constrained, weekly format
His biggest move isn't a sentence trick — it's that **the format is the brand**. Not "a newsletter,"
but the **"One Minute Blog"**: a named product with a constraint and a promise, shipped every Friday. He
even states the read-cost inside the post:

> "This is a One Minute Blog (OMB). The average person can read 238 words per minute. This blog is 459
> words, because…um… you're not average :)" — *mispriced assets*

The constraint is a default, not a cage — he breaks it on purpose and announces it ("sometimes you just
need a double stuffed Oreo," the 22-point *elon musk* essay). **For Sid:** the steal is *packaging a
named, repeatable content product with an explicit promise.*

### The post skeleton (the most copyable asset)
Nearly every essay runs the same five beats:

1. **Hook** — a question, a contrarian claim, or a curiosity gap (line 1 never mentions the offer).
2. **Story engine** — 2–4 *concrete* stories/examples carry the argument. He almost never argues abstractly.
3. **Borrowed authority** — a famous quote vouches for the idea.
4. **Name the concept** — distill the lesson into a coined, sticky handle.
5. **Turn it on the reader** — a direct challenge + sign-off.

It's **Story → Principle → Coined term → Reader challenge.** Use it for LinkedIn posts and newsletters.

### Hook menu
| Hook type | Verbatim example | Essay |
|---|---|---|
| Reframe one-liner | "Life changes once you realize everything is an excuse." | excuses |
| Challenge a cliché | "'Hard work is the key to success.' Is it?" | stop working so hard |
| Timely / "Did you see…?" | "Did you see that new Slate truck?" | underdo the competition |
| Quiz the reader | "Here's a fun question: who's the most important voice in your life?" | power brokers |
| Tease a one-word answer | "What is the number one thing you should look for in a partner?" → "down" | down |
| 2nd-person mirror | "Let's see if this describes you:" | someday |
| Cold-truth declaration | "Lemme crack open a cold-hard-truth for you…" | safe path |
| Reader-letter Q&A | opens by quoting an email from "Abram from Nashville" | proximity is power |
| Persona skit | "I'm Shaan the Seer™ — The Oracle of the East Bay." | the k-shaped economy |

### Signature devices
- **Story-first teaching** — the unit of persuasion is a named character in a specific moment.
- **Coined, ownable vocabulary** — "mispriced assets," "Ramen Era," "binge bank," "the Elon Method,"
  "Permission to Wander," "down." (Cross-ref voice-principles "named concepts as compression handles" —
  one per piece, earns its keep, introduced *after* the phenomenon.)
- **Borrowed authority as proof** — Picasso, Buffett, Naval, Paul Graham, Tim Ferriss.
- **Self-referential proof** — read-time counters, the OMB popularity claim, admitting the ChatGPT bit.
- **Cross-linking his own body of work** — explicit Part I / Part II series; recurring callbacks build a
  "binge bank" of connected pieces.

### The funnel (context, not copy craft)
Every essay ends "Want more stories like this in your inbox?" — free essay (value) → newsletter capture →
audience → monetize the audience. He documents his own playbook in *i got internet famous…* ("build a
magnet, not an audience"; "people don't want information, they want a feeling"). Full funnel map lives in
the swipe analysis; for funnel *strategy* see [persuasion-playbook.md](persuasion-playbook.md).

## Setting it to Sid's register (adapt, don't transplant)

Same moves, different voice. This is *not* a list of bans — it's how to season for **this** voice.

- **Slang / profanity → clean spoken register.** Keep the directness and the contractions; drop "risk it
  for the biscuit" and the cussing. Target: a sharp friend explaining it clearly, not a bro.
- **Celebrity / sports palette → Sid's world.** Swap NBA and Elon for music, media, AI/agents, and the
  real work of running the practice. Borrowed authority still works — just from names Sid's reader respects.
- **Comparisons → still gated.** A deliberate, clarifying image is seasoning; a reflexive metaphor is
  slop. Run the permission test every time (anti-slop). Don't out-Shaan Shaan on analogy volume.
- **Reframe constructions → rewrite to the positive claim.** Shaan uses light "what most people do / what
  to do instead" turns. In Sid's voice those read as negative parallelism (anti-slop bans it). State the
  positive claim directly.

The throughline: humanity is *deliberate imperfection* — the tangent, the fragment, the real thought,
the exact detail. Add it on purpose, on top of a clean foundation. That's seasoning. The moment it goes
reflexive and empty, it's slop again.
